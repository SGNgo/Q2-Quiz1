Sisig
1. Prepare the Pork: Boil the pork belly for 20–30 minutes, until tender. Then grill or fry until crispy and golden brown. Once cooked, chop into small pieces. 2. Sauté: In a pan over medium heat, sauté the onion until translucent. Add the chopped pork and cook until it’s browned and crispy. 3. Season: Add soy sauce and calamansi juice. Stir well and season with salt and pepper to taste. Add chilies for extra spice if desired. 4. Serve: Crack an egg on top and mix it in while the pan is still hot, or serve with a side of mayonnaise for added richness.		 The Origin

Sisig originated in Angeles City, Pampanga, Luzon. It began as a sour Kapampangan salad made with green papaya or other fruits and vinegar. In the 1970s, Lucia Cunanan popularized the now-famous version made with grilled pig’s head, chopped and served sizzling with onions, vinegar, and chilies. This version became a Filipino favorite, enjoyed across the country.
Kare-Kare
1. Boil the Meat: Simmer oxtail in water for 1–2 hours until tender. Set aside the broth.2. Add Peanut Flavor: In a pot, mix peanut butter with annatto powder and some broth. Stir until thick. 3. Add Vegetables: Add banana heart, string beans, and pechay; cook until tender. 4. Season: Stir in fish sauce, salt, and pepper. 5. Serve: Enjoy with rice and bagoong on the side.		 The Origin

Kare-kare originated in Luzon, especially in Pampanga. It’s thought to be influenced by Indian curry, brought by Indian soldiers during British rule in Manila, or created by Kapampangans using local ingredients, with its unique peanut-based sauce setting it apart.
Adobo
1. Marinate: Combine meat, soy sauce, garlic, and peppercorns. Marinate for 30 minutes. 2. Simmer: In a pot, add marinated meat, vinegar, bay leaf, and water. 3.Cook: Simmer until meat is tender and sauce is slightly thickened. 4. Serve: Enjoy with rice! 		The Origin

Adobo originated in the Philippines as a method of preserving meat by marinating it in vinegar, salt, and spices. When Spanish colonizers arrived, they named the dish "adobo" after a similar Spanish marinade, but the Filipino version remained unique with local ingredients like soy sauce and garlic.
Longanissa
1. Mix: In a bowl, combine pork, sugar, garlic, vinegar, soy sauce, pepper, and salt. Mix well. 2. Shape: Form into small sausages, or stuff into casings if available. 3. Cook: In a pan, add a bit of water and cook longganisa over medium heat until water evaporates. Fry until golden brown. 4.Serve: Enjoy with garlic rice and a fried egg! 		The Origin

Longganisa is believed to have originated in the Philippines during the Spanish colonial period. The dish is a Filipino version of the Spanish "chorizo", which influenced the Filipino sausage-making tradition. Different regions in the Philippines have their own variations of longganisa, with distinct flavors—such as the sweet "Vigan longganisa" from Ilocos and the garlicky "Lucban longganisa" from Quezon.
Sinigang
1. Boil Meat: In a pot, add pork (or your choice of protein) with water. Bring to a boil and simmer until tender. 2. Add Vegetables: Add onion, tomatoes, and vegetables. Continue to simmer until vegetables are tender. 3. Season: Stir in tamarind paste or sinigang mix for the sour flavor. Add fish sauce, salt, and pepper to taste. 4. Serve: Serve hot with steamed rice. 		The Origin

Sinigang is a traditional Filipino dish with roots in pre-colonial Filipino cooking. The dish is believed to have been influenced by the use of souring agents like tamarind, which is native to the Philippines. Over time, it evolved into a popular sour soup or stew, with various regional variations using different souring ingredients such as green mango or calamansi.
